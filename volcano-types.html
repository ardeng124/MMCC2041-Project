There are four basic types of volcanoes; cinder cones, composite volcanoes, shield volcanoes, and lava domes.

Cinder cones

Not only the most common type of volcanic formation, but also the simplest and smallest.


Cinder cones consist of fragments of ash or cinders that have built up from eruptions over time.


They are often symmetrical and circular and have slopes at 30-40° with a bowl shaped crater at the top.


One of the most famous cinder cones is a volcano in Mexico called Paracutin.

Composite volcanoes

Composite volcanoes, also known as stratovolcanoes are formed by many layers of lava, ash, and rocks piling up on top of each other after eruptions.


Due to the composition of these volcanoes there are many vents inside which lava can erupt from, as well as a main crater.


Composite volcanoes are most commonly formed in subduction zones (convergent plates).

Shield volcanoes

Shield volcanoes are wide, low profile volcanoes. They from through the build-up of lava from eruptions, and due to the very low viscosity of the lava, the flow is much thinner further out from the crater.


As a result shield volcanoes form with very wide and shallow sloping sides.


Shield volcanoes are most notably known for making up a handful of the Hawaiian islands. The worlds most active volcano, Kīlauea, is a shield volcano on the main Hawaiian island. Shield volcanoes are formed by hotspots.

Lava domes

Lava domes are formed by highly viscous lava that cannot flow away from the vent.

They often form inside the craters of existing volcanoes and are most common in convergent plate boundaries.

They sit at the opening of the vent because the lava does not have enough pressure to erupt explosively, and with the lava also being very viscous the flow doesn’t leave the vent.
